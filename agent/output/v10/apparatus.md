- trbh [496]: `ādau nirdiṣṭatvād ādyāḥ sarvatragā ity arthaḥ` — Because they were indicated at the beginning, they are ‘initial’; the meaning is ‘omnipresent.’
- trbh [498, 499]: `sadāsparśamanaskāravitsaṃjñācetanānvitam
iti prathamato nirdiṣṭāḥ` — The earlier quoted phrase shows that they were indicated first.

**J2. Analyze the compound as bahuvrīhi and translate ‘those beginning with contact,’ not merely ‘contact and so on’ without its category function.**
- chosen: sparśādayaḥ is a bahuvrīhi: ‘those whose first is contact,’ i.e. the five omnipresent factors beginning with sparśa. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [500]: `sparśa eṣām ādir iti sparśādayaḥ` — Contact is their beginning; therefore they are ‘those beginning with contact.’
- trbh [501, 502]: `te punaḥ
sparśamanaskārādayaḥ pañca dharmāḥ sarvacittam anugacchantīti sarvatragāḥ` — Those, again, are the five dharmas beginning with contact and attention; because they accompany every mind, they are omnipresent.
- trbh [503, 504]: `tathā hy eta ālayavijñāne kliṣṭe manasi pravṛttivijñāneṣu cāviśeṣeṇa
pravartante` — For these occur without distinction in ālayavijñāna, in the afflicted mind, and in the active consciousnesses.

**J3. Treat chandādhimokṣasmṛtayaḥ as a three-member dvandva and samādhidhībhyām as a two-member instrumental dual dvandva joined by saha.**
- chosen: chandādhimokṣasmṛtayaḥ saha samādhidhībhyām enumerates five distinct viniyata dharmas; saha governs the instrumental dual and does not assert that they always co-occur. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [505, 506, 507]: `viniyatān adhikṛtyāha
chandādhimokṣasmṛtayaḥ saha
samādhidhībhyāṃ niyatāḥ` — With regard to the determinate factors he says: desire, resolve, and mindfulness, together with samādhi and dhī, are niyata.
- trbh [568, 569, 570, 571]: `ete hi pañca dharmāḥ parasparaṃ vyatiricyāpi pravartante
evañ ca
yatrādhimokṣas tatra nāvaśyam itarair api bhavitavyam
evaṃ sarvatra vācyam` — For these five dharmas occur even while distinct from one another. Thus, where adhimokṣa is present, the others need not necessarily also be present; this should be said in every case.

**J4. Translate niyatāḥ as ‘object-specific determinate factors’ because the commentary defines them by restriction to a special object, not by universal occurrence.**
- chosen: niyatāḥ is the technical class viniyatāḥ, ‘determinate/object-specific,’ not ‘necessary’ or merely ‘restrained.’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [508, 509]: `viśeṣe niyatatvād viniyatāḥ
eṣāṃ hi viśeṣa eva viṣayo na sarvaḥ` — They are viniyata because they are determined with respect to a specific case; for their object is precisely a specific one, not every object.
- trbh [572]: `uktā viniyatāḥ` — The determinate factors have been stated.

**J5. Use technical English glosses constrained by the commentary rather than dictionary defaults such as ‘meter’ for chanda, ‘tradition’ for smṛti, or ‘meditation’ alone for samādhi.**
- chosen: Translate the five viniyata terms according to Sthiramati’s definitions: chanda as desire/aspiration, adhimokṣa as resolve/ascertainment, smṛti as mindfulness/recollection, samādhi as concentration, and dhī as discernment/prajñā. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [510]: `tatra chando 'bhiprete vastuny abhilāṣaḥ` — Here chanda is desire for an intended object.
- trbh [515]: `darśanaśravaṇādiprārthanā chandaḥ` — Chanda is the aspiration for seeing, hearing, and the like.
- trbh [517]: `adhimokṣo niścite vastuni tathaivāvadhāraṇam` — Adhimokṣa is ascertainment just so with respect to a settled object.
- trbh [524, 525]: `evam etan nānyathety avadhāraṇam
adhimokṣaḥ` — The ascertainment ‘this is so, not otherwise’ is adhimokṣa.
- trbh [529]: `smṛtiḥ saṃstute vastuny asaṃpramoṣaś cetaso 'bhilapanatā` — Smṛti is non-loss with respect to a familiar object, the mind’s verbal noting.
- trbh [533, 534]: `pūrvagṛhītasya vastunaḥ punaḥ punar ālambanākārasmaraṇam abhilapanatā
abhilapanam evābhilapanatā` — Verbal noting is repeatedly remembering the object-aspect of a previously apprehended thing; verbal noting itself is verbal-noting-ness.
- trbh [538, 539, 540, 541]: `samādhir upaparīkṣye vastuni cittasyaikāgratā
upaparīkṣyaṃ vastu guṇato
doṣato vā
ekāgratā ekālambanatā` — Samādhi is the one-pointedness of mind on an object to be examined, whether by its qualities or defects; one-pointedness is having one object.
- trbh [545, 546, 547, 548, 549, 550]: `dhīḥ prajñā
sāpy upaparīkṣya eva vastuni pravicayo yogāyogavihito 'nyathā
veti
pravicinotīti pravicayaḥ
yaḥ samyaṅ mithyā vā
saṃkīrṇasvasāmānyalakṣaṇeṣv iva dharmeṣu vivekāvabodhaḥ` — Dhī is prajñā. It too, with respect to an object to be examined, is analysis produced by correct method, by incorrect method, or otherwise. Analysis is so called because one analyzes; whether correct or mistaken, it is discriminative understanding of dharmas with mixed own- and common-characteristics.

**J6. Do not include śraddhā, hrī, and apatrapā among the five niyata factors; translate them as the next, wholesome, items.**
- chosen: śraddhā atha hrīḥ apatrapā starts the kuśala group; atha marks transition after the viniyata factors. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [572, 573, 574]: `uktā viniyatāḥ
tadanantaroddiṣṭās tv idānīṃ kuśalā vaktavyā ity ata āha
śraddhātha hrīr apatrapā` — The determinate factors have been stated. Now the wholesome factors indicated immediately after them are to be stated; therefore he says: ‘faith, then shame, apatrapā.’

## Translation

> The initial ones (ādyāḥ)—those beginning with contact (sparśa)—are the omnipresent (sarvatraga);
desire (chanda), resolve (adhimokṣa), and mindfulness (smṛti),
together with concentration (samādhi) and discernment (dhī/prajñā), are the object-specific determinate factors (niyata/viniyata);
then come faith (śraddhā), shame (hrī), and compunction (apatrapā).
