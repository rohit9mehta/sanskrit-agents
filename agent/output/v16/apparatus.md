## Justifications

**J1. Construe manovijñānasambhūtiḥ as the nominal subject/predicate of an understood rule: mental consciousness occurs/arises in all states except the listed exceptions.**
- chosen: ‘The arising/occurrence of mental consciousness’ rather than a bare metaphysical ‘birth’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1081, 1082]: `etāḥ pañcāvasthā varjayitvā tadanyāsu sarvāsv avasthāsu manovijñānapravṛttir
veditavyā` — Excluding these five states, in all other states the occurrence/activity of mental consciousness is to be understood.
- trbh [1083, 1084, 1085, 1086]: `evam āsaṃjñikādiṣu manovijñāne niruddhe tadapagame punaḥ kuta
utpadyate yatas tasya kālakriyā na bhavati
tat punar ālayavijñānād
evotpadyate` — When mental consciousness has ceased in the āsaṃjñika and the rest, whence does it arise again when that has passed, since it has no temporal efficacy then? It arises only from the store-consciousness.

**J2. sarvadā is not merely vague ‘always’; the commentary explicitly expands it as all times both accompanied by and not accompanied by the visual and other sense-consciousnesses.**
- chosen: ‘at all times, whether together with or without eye-consciousnesses and the rest’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1055]: `sarvadeti sarvakālaṃ cakṣurādivijñānaiḥ saha vinā cety arthaḥ` — ‘Always’ means ‘at all times, both together with eye-consciousnesses and the rest, and without them.’

**J3. The ablatives after ṛte are construed as exceptions to the general rule, not as sources from which mental consciousness arises. The commentary calls them an apavāda and later says that these five states are to be excluded.**
- chosen: ṛte introduces an exception-list of five states (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1056, 1057, 1058]: `asyotsargasyemam apavādam ārabhate
āsaṃjñikād ṛte
samāpattidvayān middhān mūrcchanād apy acittakād iti` — He begins this exception to the general rule: ‘except for the āsaṃjñika, the two attainments, middha, and also mindless fainting.’
- trbh [1081, 1082]: `etāḥ pañcāvasthā varjayitvā tadanyāsu sarvāsv avasthāsu manovijñānapravṛttir
veditavyā` — Excluding these five states, in all other states the occurrence of mental consciousness is to be understood.

**J4. Do not render āsaṃjñika as a generic adjective ‘unconscious’; it is a named state in which cittacaitasika dharmas cease for beings born among the unconscious gods.**
- chosen: āsaṃjñika as a technical unconscious state involving cessation of mind and mental factors among asaṃjñisattva gods (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1059, 1060]: `tatrāsaṃjñikam asaṃjñisattveṣu deveṣūpapannasya yaś cittacaitasikānāṃ
dharmāṇāṃ nirodhaḥ` — Here the āsaṃjñika is the cessation of mental and mental-associated dharmas in one born among the gods who are unconscious beings.

**J5. samāpattidvaya is not ‘two meetings/accidents’; the commentary names the two contemplative attainments and defines them by cessation of manovijñāna, with further cessation of kliṣṭa manas in nirodhasamāpatti.**
- chosen: samāpattidvaya as ‘the two attainments’: asaṃjñisamāpatti and nirodhasamāpatti (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1061]: `samāpattidvayam asaṃjñisamāpattir nirodhasamāpattiś ca` — The pair of attainments is the unconscious attainment and the cessation attainment.
- trbh [1064, 1065]: `niḥsaraṇasaṃjñāpūrvakeṇa manasikāreṇa manovijñānasya tatsaṃprayuktānāñ ca
caittānāṃ yo nirodhaḥ so 'trāsaṃjñisamāpattir ity ucyate` — The cessation of mental consciousness and its associated mental factors, through attention preceded by the notion of escape, is here called the unconscious attainment.
- trbh [1072, 1073, 1074]: `nirodhasamāpattir ākiñcanyāyatanavītarāgasya śāntavihārasaṃjñāpūrvakeṇa
manasikāreṇa sasaṃprayogasya manovijñānasya kliṣṭasya ca manaso yo nirodhaḥ
iyam apy asaṃjñisamāpattivad āśrayasyāvasthāviśeṣe prajñapyate` — The cessation attainment is the cessation, for one free from attachment to the sphere of nothingness, of mental consciousness together with its associates and of the afflicted manas, through attention preceded by the notion of peaceful abiding; like the unconscious attainment, it is designated upon a particular condition of the basis.

**J6. The verse’s middha is the exceptional acittaka kind: because the basis is impaired by intense middha, mental consciousness does not occur for that time.**
- chosen: middha as mindless torpor/sleep, not ordinary sloth alone (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1075, 1076]: `acittakaṃ middhaṃ gāḍhamiddhopahatatvād āśrayasya tāvatkālaṃ
manovijñānāpravṛtter acittakam ity ucyate` — Middha is mindless: because the basis is impaired by intense middha, for that time mental consciousness does not occur; therefore it is called mindless.

**J7. Translate mūrcchanāt as fainting/syncope, not ‘stupefying’ or another sense of mūrchana; the commentary defines it as a mindless condition produced by external blow or humoral imbalance and opposed to the occurrence of mental consciousness.**
- chosen: mūrcchanā/mūrcchā as mindless fainting or syncope (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1077, 1078, 1079, 1080]: `acittikā mūrcchā
āgantukenābhighātena vātapittaśleṣmavaiṣamyeṇa vā yad
āśrayavaiṣamyaṃ
manovijñānapravṛttiviruddhaṃ tatrācittikā mūrcchopacaryate` — Fainting is mindless. Where there is an imbalance of the basis, caused by an external blow or by imbalance of wind, bile, and phlegm, opposed to the occurrence of mental consciousness, there mindless fainting is figuratively designated.

## Translation

> The arising of mental consciousness (manovijñāna)
is at all times—whether with the eye-consciousnesses and the rest, or without them—
except in the unconscious state (āsaṃjñika),
in the two attainments (samāpatti-dvaya),
in mindless torpor (acittaka middha),
and also in mindless fainting (mūrcchā).

- It might render sambhūti as bare ‘birth/origin’; the commentary’s manovijñānapravṛttiḥ veditavyā supports ‘occurrence/arising’ within the rule about when manovijñāna is active.
