## Justifications

**J1. Translate the opening as ‘the dependent nature is construction.’ The bhāṣya says that vikalpa states the paratantra’s own form and pratyayodbhava states the reason for the designation paratantra.**
- chosen: paratantrasvabhāvaḥ is the dependent nature being defined as vikalpa, not merely ‘a construction whose nature is dependent.’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1320, 1321, 1322]: `atra ca vikalpa iti paratantrasvarūpam āha
pratyayodbhava ity anenāpi
paratantrābhidhānapravṛttinimittam āha` — Here, by ‘vikalpa’ he states the dependent’s own form; by ‘born from conditions’ too he states the basis for applying the designation ‘dependent.’

**J2. Render vikalpa as ‘construction’ and identify its scope as citta-caittas, not as ordinary ‘alternative/option.’**
- chosen: vikalpa is Yogācāra constructive imagination/false construction, concretely the mind and mental factors of the three realms. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1323, 1324, 1325, 1326]: `tatra parikalpaḥ
kuśalākuśalāvyākṛtabhedabhinnās traidhātukāś cittacaittāḥ
yathoktam
abhūtaparikalpas tu cittacaittās tridhātukā iti` — There, construction is the mind and mental factors belonging to the three realms, differentiated as wholesome, unwholesome, and indeterminate. As has been said: ‘False construction is the mind and mental factors of the three realms.’

**J3. Read pratyaya in its Buddhist causal sense, because Sthiramati glosses it with hetupratyaya and explains paratantra as having its self-attainment bound to other causes and conditions.**
- chosen: pratyayodbhava means ‘born/produced from causes and conditions.’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1327, 1328, 1329]: `parair hetupratyayais tantryata iti paratantra utpādyata ity arthaḥ
svato
'nyahetupratyayapratibaddhātmalābha iti yāvaduktaṃ bhavati` — It is paratantra because it is governed by other causes and conditions; the meaning is ‘produced.’ In short, its acquisition of its own being is bound to causes and conditions other than itself.

**J4. Translate niṣpannaḥ as ‘the perfected nature,’ since the commentary introduces the question about pariniṣpanna and concludes with pariniṣpannasvabhāvaḥ; its mode is avikārapariniṣpatti, unchanging consummation.**
- chosen: niṣpannaḥ is the pariniṣpanna/pariniṣpannasvabhāva, the perfected nature, not an ordinary produced result. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1331, 1332, 1333]: `pariniṣpannaḥ katham ity ata āha
niṣpannas tasya pūrveṇa sadā rahitatā tu yā
avikārapariniṣpattyā sa pariniṣpannaḥ` — How is the perfected to be stated? Therefore he says: ‘The perfected is whatever is its always being devoid of the former.’ Because of unchanging consummation, it is perfected.
- trbh [1340]: `sa pariniṣpannasvabhāvaḥ` — That is the perfected nature.

**J5. Translate ‘its being devoid of the former’ as the dependent nature’s being devoid of the imagined subject–object duality, not as a temporal phrase ‘by/before the previous one.’**
- chosen: tasya refers to the paratantra; pūrveṇa refers to the former parikalpita, specifically the imagined grasper–grasped structure in vikalpa. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1334, 1335, 1336, 1337, 1338]: `tasyeti paratantrasya pūrveṇeti
parikalpitena tasmin vikalpe grāhyagrāhakabhāvaḥ parikalpitaḥ
tathā hi
tasmin vikalpe grāhyagrāhakatvam avidyamānam eva parikalpyata iti parikalpitam
ucyate` — ‘Of that’ means of the dependent; ‘by the former’ means by the imagined. In that construction the state of grasped and grasper is imagined. For so it is: in that construction, grasped-and-grasper-hood, though precisely nonexistent, is imagined; therefore it is called imagined.

**J6. Render rahitatā as ‘being devoid/absence’ and sadā as ‘always,’ strengthened by the bhāṣya’s sarvakālam and atyantarahitatā.**
- chosen: sadā rahitatā is constant and absolute devoidness/absence of the grasper–grasped in the dependent nature. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1339, 1340]: `tena grāhyagrāhakeṇa paratantrasya sadā sarvakālam atyantarahitatā yā
sa pariniṣpannasvabhāvaḥ` — The constant, at-all-times, absolute devoidness of the dependent from that grasped-and-grasper—that is the perfected nature.

## Translation

> But the dependent nature (paratantra-svabhāva)
is construction (vikalpa), born of conditions (pratyaya).
The perfected nature (pariniṣpanna-svabhāva) is
its always being devoid of the former—
the imagined grasper-and-grasped.

## One-shot delta

- Without the bhāṣya, vikalpa might be rendered as ordinary ‘option’ or ‘alternative’; Sthiramati instead makes it the Yogācāra false construction consisting of three-realm mind and mental factors.
- Without the bhāṣya, pratyaya might be taken as ‘belief’ or ‘notion’; Sthiramati’s hetupratyaya explanation fixes it as causal conditions.
- Without the bhāṣya, tasya and pūrveṇa could be taken mechanically as ‘of it’ and ‘by/before the former’; Sthiramati specifies: ‘of the dependent’ and ‘of the imagined,’ namely grasper–grasped duality in vikalpa.
- Without the bhāṣya, niṣpannaḥ could sound like an ordinary produced/accomplished thing; Sthiramati identifies it with the pariniṣpanna-svabhāva and defines it through unchanging consummation and absolute absence.
