## Justifications

**J1. tasya vyāvṛttir arhattve is not translated as an abstract ‘exclusion of that from arhatship’ or as a merely logical ‘distinction’; it states that ālayavijñāna has ceased/turned away in the state of arhatship.**
- chosen: ‘Its cessation/turning-away is in arhatship’—the genitive tasya refers to ālayavijñāna, and vyāvṛtti is its cessation in the arhat-state. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [345, 346, 349, 350, 351, 352]: `kiṃ punar arhattvam
yadyogād arhann ity ucyate
kṣayajñānānutpādajñānalābhāt
tasyāṃ hy avasthāyām
ālayavijñānāśritadauṣṭhulyaniravaśeṣaprahāṇād ālayavijñānaṃ vyāvṛttaṃ bhavati
saiva cārhadavasthā` — What is arhatship? It is that by connection with which one is called an arhat: by the attainment of the knowledge of destruction and the knowledge of non-arising. For in that state, because of the complete abandonment of the coarseness resting on ālayavijñāna, ālayavijñāna has turned away/ceased; that alone is the arhat-state.

**J2. The pronoun tad is not allowed to refer to arhattva, vyāvṛtti, or an unspecified prior item; Sthiramati explicitly binds it to ālayavijñāna. The verb is rendered ‘proceeds/arises’, not ‘sets itself into activity’ in a transitive sense.**
- chosen: tad in tad āśritya refers to ālayavijñāna, and pravartate means that the defiled manas arises/proceeds in a continuum relying on it. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [363, 364, 365]: `tad āśritya pravartata iti tacchabdenālayavijñānam abhisaṃbadhyate
tadvāsanāśrayo hy ālayavijñānam atas tad āśritya pravartate santānenotpadyata
ity arthaḥ` — In ‘relying on that it proceeds’, the word ‘that’ is connected with ālayavijñāna. For ālayavijñāna is the basis of its latent impressions; therefore ‘relying on that it proceeds’ means ‘it arises in a continuum’.
- trbh [366, 367, 368]: `atha vā yasmin dhātau bhūmau vālayavijñānaṃ vipākas tad api
kliṣṭaṃ manas taddhātukaṃ tadbhūmikaṃ ceti tatpratibaddhavṛttitvāt tad āśritya
pravartate` — Or else, in whatever realm or level ālayavijñāna exists as maturation, that defiled manas too belongs to that realm and that level; because its occurrence is bound to that, it proceeds relying on that.

**J3. The compound is read as an adjectival compound qualifying vijñānam/manas, and its tad again points to ālayavijñāna; ālamba is interpreted in the Yogācāra technical sense of object-support, not merely a physical prop.**
- chosen: tadālambam means ‘having ālayavijñāna as its object-support (ālambana)’. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [369, 370]: `tadālambam ity ālayavijñānālambanam eva satkāyadṛṣṭyādibhiḥ saṃprayogād ahaṃ
mamety ālayavijñānālambanatvāt` — ‘Having that as its object’ means precisely having ālayavijñāna as its object, because it is associated with satkāyadṛṣṭi and the others, since in thinking ‘I’ and ‘mine’ it has ālayavijñāna as its object.
- trbh [371, 372, 373, 374]: `kathaṃ punar yata eva cittād utpadyate tad
eva tadālambanaṃ bhavati
yathā tad anicchatāṃ keṣāṃcit kasyāñcid avasthāyāṃ
yata eva cittān manovijñānam utpadyate tadālambanam eva tad utpadyate` — How, then, can the very mind from which it arises be what it has as object? Just as, for some who do not accept that, in a certain state the mental consciousness arises from a mind and arises with that very mind as its object.

**J4. manonāma is not two separate syntactic words ‘mind, indeed/name’; it is a designation of the second transformation as a consciousness called manas, distinguished from ālayavijñāna and the active cognitions.**
- chosen: manonāma vijñānam means ‘a consciousness whose name is manas’. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [375, 376, 377]: `manonāma vijñānam iti mana iti nāma ākhyā yasya vijñānasya tad ālayavijñānam
āśritya pravarte tadālambanaṃ ca manonāmety anenālayavijñānāt pravṛttivijñānāc
ca vyavacchinatti` — ‘A consciousness named manas’ means: that consciousness whose name or appellation is ‘manas’. By saying that it relies on ālayavijñāna, has that as its object, and is named manas, he distinguishes it from ālayavijñāna and from the active consciousnesses.

**J5. The final adjective states the svabhāva/nature of this consciousness; it is not merely an incidental ‘thoughtful’ quality. The derivation of the name manas is from manana by a nirukta-style explanation.**
- chosen: mananātmakam means ‘having cogitation/reflection as its nature’, explaining why it is called manas. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [378, 379, 381, 382]: `tat punaḥ kiṃ svabhāvam ity āha
mananātmakam iti
mananātmakatvān mana ity ucyate nairūktena vidhinā
vijñānasvarūpatvād avaśyaṃ tac caittaiḥ saṃprayujyate` — What, then, is its own nature? He says: ‘having cogitation as its nature.’ Because it has cogitation as its nature, it is called manas by the etymological method. Since it has the nature of consciousness, it is necessarily associated with mental factors.

**J6. Although this consciousness relies on and takes ālayavijñāna as object, it is not identified with ālayavijñāna; the commentary introduces it as the second transformation called manana and explicitly distinguishes it from both ālayavijñāna and pravṛttivijñāna.**
- chosen: The second half names the defiled manas as the second transformation, not ālayavijñāna itself. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [353, 354]: `uktaḥ savibhaṅgo vipākapariṇāmaḥ
idānīṃ mananākhyāṃ dvitīyaṃ pariṇāmam āha` — The maturation-transformation has been explained with its divisions. Now he states the second transformation, called manana.
- trbh [357, 358, 359, 360]: `tatra yathā cakṣurādivijñānānāṃ cakṣurādaya āśrayatvena rūpādayaś
cālambanatvena prasiddhā naiva kliṣṭasya manasa āśraya ālambanaṃ vā prasiddham

And a consciousness cannot be without dependence on a support and an object; therefore, in order to explain the support and object of the defiled manas, and to explain the etymology, he says...

## Translation

> Its cessation is in arhatship (arhattva).
Relying on that—the storehouse consciousness (ālayavijñāna)—it proceeds.
Having that as its object-support (ālambana),
it is a consciousness (vijñāna) named mind (manas),
whose nature is cogitation (manana).

## Analyzer disagreements

- arhattve: ByT5 segmented arhat + tve and treated tva separately; the bhāṣya and commentary read a single locative singular noun arhattva, as shown by kiṃ punar arhattvam at trbh 345 and the verse reading arhattve.
- tadālambaṃ: ByT5 split tad + ālambam for segmentation; here it is treated as one inflected compound stem tadālamba, because the commentary glosses tadālambam as ālayavijñānālambanam at trbh 369.
- manonāma: ByT5 split manaḥ + nāma and appears to take manaḥ as a separate neuter subanta plus nāma; the commentary explicitly analyzes the phrase as a single bahuvrīhi-like designation: mana iti nāma ākhyā yasya vijñānasya at trbh 375.
- mananātmakam: ByT5 segmented manana + ātmakam; here it is treated as the single compound stem mananātmaka, supported by the commentary’s mananātmakatvān mana ity ucyate at trbh 381.
- vijñānaṃ and its agreeing adjectives tadālambaṃ, manonāma, mananātmakam are taken syntactically as nominative singular, not accusative, because they supply the subject of pravartate.

## One-shot delta

- A commentary-blind translation might take tasya in the second pāda as referring to arhatship or to the immediately preceding vyāvṛtti; Sthiramati says both tad in āśritya and tad in tadālambam refer to ālayavijñāna.
- It might translate vyāvṛtti as a general ‘distinction’ or ‘exclusion’; the bhāṣya makes it the cessation/turning-away of ālayavijñāna in the arhat-state after complete abandonment of ālayavijñāna-based dauṣṭhulya.
- It might read manonāma as two words, ‘mind, indeed’, or as a loose name; the commentary parses it as ‘the consciousness whose name/appellation is manas’.
- It might miss that the verse has shifted from the first transformation to the second transformation, the defiled manas; Sthiramati explicitly marks this transition.
- It might render ālamba as only ‘support’ and duplicate āśritya; Sthiramati distinguishes āśraya and ālambana and glosses tadālambam as ālayavijñāna-ālambanam, object-directed toward ālayavijñāna in the ‘I’ and ‘mine’ appropriation.
