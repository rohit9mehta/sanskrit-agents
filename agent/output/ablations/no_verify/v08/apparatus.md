**J1. The verse is not saying that ‘this second transformation is third’; it closes the second transformation and then begins the third. The masculine tṛtīyaḥ is construed with the supplied masculine vijñānapariṇāmaḥ.**
- chosen: Take dvitīyaḥ pariṇāmo 'yam as a concluding sentence about the second transformation, and take tṛtīyaḥ as introducing the third consciousness-transformation with supplied vijñānapariṇāmaḥ. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [473, 474]: `uddiṣṭo nidiṣṭaś ceti nigamayati
dvitīyapariṇāmāntaraṃ tṛtīyaḥ pariṇāmo vaktavya ity ata āha` — He concludes that what was stated has also been explained. Since, after the second transformation, the third transformation remains to be stated, therefore he says...
- trbh [475, 476, 477]: `tṛtīyaḥ ṣaḍvidhasya yā
viṣayasyopalabdhiḥ sā
tṛtīyo vijñānapariṇāma iti vākyaśeṣaḥ` — ‘The third: whatever apprehension there is of the sixfold object, that...’ The remainder of the sentence is: ‘the third consciousness-transformation.’

**J2. The genitive phrase is ‘of the sixfold object,’ not ‘of a sixfold apprehension’ or an unspecified sixfold consciousness.**
- chosen: ṣaḍvidhasya qualifies viṣayasya: the object is sixfold, namely the domains whose nature is form, sound, smell, taste, tangible, and dharma. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [478, 479]: `ṣaḍvidhasyeti ṣaṭprakārasya
rūpaśabdagandharasaspraṣṭavya dharmātmakasya viṣayasya yā upalabdhir grahaṇaṃ` — ‘Sixfold’ means ‘of six kinds’: whatever apprehension, grasping, there is of an object whose nature is form, sound, smell, taste, tangible, and dharma...

**J3. The bhāṣya glosses upalabdhi with grahaṇa and pratipatti, so the relevant sense is perceptual/cognitive taking-up, not acquisition or possession.**
- chosen: Translate upalabdhiḥ as ‘apprehension’ and construe viṣayasya as its objective genitive: apprehension of the object. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [479, 480]: `rūpaśabdagandharasaspraṣṭavya dharmātmakasya viṣayasya yā upalabdhir grahaṇaṃ
pratipattir ity arthaḥ` — Whatever apprehension of the object whose nature is form, sound, smell, taste, tangible, and dharma—‘apprehension’ means grasping; the sense is cognition/recognition.

**J4. The pronouns frame the apprehension as the subject subsequently classified ethically.**
- chosen: Read yā ... sā as a relative-correlative construction whose referent is the feminine upalabdhiḥ. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [475, 476, 481]: `tṛtīyaḥ ṣaḍvidhasya yā
viṣayasyopalabdhiḥ sā
sā punaḥ kiṃ kuśalākuśalāvyākṛtety ata āha` — ‘Whatever apprehension there is of the sixfold object, that...’ Again, is that wholesome, unwholesome, or undetermined? Therefore he says...

**J5. Sthiramati first restates the question as the standard triad kuśala/akuśala/avyākṛta, then glosses advayā as avyākṛtā and as ‘not wholesome, not unwholesome.’**
- chosen: Translate kuśalā akuśalā advayā as ‘wholesome, unwholesome, or neither,’ with advayā meaning the ethically undetermined (avyākṛta), not metaphysical non-duality. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [481, 482, 483]: `sā punaḥ kiṃ kuśalākuśalāvyākṛtety ata āha
kuśalākuśalādvayā
kuśalākuśalādvayety avyākṛtāpi` — Again, is it wholesome, unwholesome, or undetermined? Therefore he says: ‘wholesome, unwholesome, or neither.’ By saying ‘wholesome, unwholesome, or neither,’ the undetermined too is included.
- trbh [484, 485, 486, 487]: `alobhādveṣāmohaiḥ saṃprayuktā kuśalā
lobhadveṣamohaiḥ saṃprayuktākuśalā
kuśalākuśalair asaṃprayuktādvayā na
kuśalā nākuśalety arthaḥ` — Conjoined with non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion, it is wholesome. Conjoined with greed, hatred, and delusion, it is unwholesome. Unconjoined with wholesome and unwholesome factors, it is neither: the meaning is ‘not wholesome and not unwholesome.’

## Translation

> This is the second transformation (pariṇāma).
The third consciousness-transformation (vijñāna-pariṇāma)
is whatever apprehension (upalabdhi) there is
of the sixfold object (viṣaya):
that is wholesome (kuśala), unwholesome (akuśala),
or neither—the undetermined (advayā/avyākṛta).

## One-shot delta

- A commentary-blind translation might join dvitīyaḥ and tṛtīyaḥ into a contradictory single predication, instead of seeing that the first phrase concludes the second transformation and the rest introduces the third.
- It might take tṛtīyaḥ to agree with upalabdhiḥ; Sthiramati supplies the masculine vijñānapariṇāmaḥ.
- It might render upalabdhi as ‘acquisition’ and miss the bhāṣya’s grahaṇa/pratipatti sense: apprehension or perceptual grasping of the object.
- It might leave ‘sixfold’ vague or attach it to apprehension; Sthiramati attaches it to the object and lists the six object-types.
- It might translate advayā as ‘non-dual’; Sthiramati makes it the ethically undetermined third option, ‘not wholesome and not unwholesome.’
