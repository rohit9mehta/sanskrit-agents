## Justifications

**J1. The demonstrative does not refer simply to the immediately preceding word or to an abstract ‘that’ left unspecified; Sthiramati explicitly glosses it as the āśraya-parāvṛtti-rūpa, the transformation of the basis discussed just before.**
- chosen: saḥ eva = ‘that very basis-transformation (āśraya-parāvṛtti)’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1511, 1512, 1513, 1514, 1515, 1516]: `tasya jñānasyānantaraṃ āśrayasya parāvṛttir bhavatīti jñāpanārtham āha
āśrayasya parāvṛttir iti
āśrayo 'tra sarvabījakam ālayavijñānam
tasya parāvṛttir yā
dauṣṭhulyavipākadvayavāsanābhāvena nivṛttau satyāṃ
karmaṇyatādharmakāyādvayajñānabhāvena parāvṛttiḥ` — To indicate that, immediately after that knowledge, there is a transformation of the basis, he says ‘transformation of the basis.’ Here the basis is the store-consciousness with all seeds. Its transformation is the transformation in which, when there is cessation through absence of the impressions of both badness and maturation, there is the state of fitness, the dharma-body, and non-dual cognition.
- trbh [1537, 1538, 1539]: `sa evānāsravo dhātur iti
sa evāśrayaparāvṛttirūpaḥ
anāsravo dhātur ity ucyate nirdauṣṭhulyatvāt` — As to ‘that very thing is the uncontaminated element’: that very thing, being of the nature of basis-transformation, is called the uncontaminated element because it is free from badness.

**J2. ‘Anāsrava’ is defined as free from all āsravas, because it is free from dauṣṭhulya; ‘dhātu’ is not left as a bare cosmological element but is glossed in a causal sense, as the cause/source of noble dharmas.**
- chosen: anāsravo dhātuḥ = ‘uncontaminated element-source’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1539, 1540, 1541, 1542, 1543]: `anāsravo dhātur ity ucyate nirdauṣṭhulyatvāt
sarvāsravavigata ity anāsravaḥ
āryadharmahetutvād dhātuḥ
hetvartho hy atra
dhātuśabdaḥ` — It is called the uncontaminated element because it is free from badness. ‘Uncontaminated’ means devoid of all outflows. It is ‘dhātu’ because it is the cause of noble dharmas; here the word dhātu has the meaning ‘cause.’

**J3. The commentary grounds acintya in three reasons: it is not accessible to reasoning, it is to be known personally, and there is no example for it.**
- chosen: acintyaḥ = ‘inconceivable’ in the sense of beyond reasoning and analogy, personally realized (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1544]: `acintyas tarkāgocaratvāt pratyātmavedyatvād dṛṣṭāntābhāvāc ca` — It is inconceivable because it is beyond the range of reasoning, because it is to be known individually/personally, and because there is no example.

**J4. The term is not rendered as ‘clever’ or merely ‘skilful’; Sthiramati explains it by purity of object, safety, and constitution out of uncontaminated dharmas.**
- chosen: kuśalaḥ = ‘wholesome’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1545, 1546]: `kuśalo
viśuddhālambanatvāt kṣematvād anāsravadharmamayatvāc ca` — It is wholesome because its object is purified, because it is secure, and because it consists of uncontaminated dharmas.

**J5. Sthiramati links dhruva with nityatva and akṣayatā, and derives sukha from that very permanence by the contrast ‘whatever is impermanent is suffering; this is permanent.’**
- chosen: dhruvaḥ and sukhaḥ = ‘constant’ and ‘blissful’ because permanent/inexhaustible (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1547, 1548, 1549, 1550]: `dhruvo nityatvād
akṣayatayā
sukho nityatvād eva yad anityaṃ tad duḥkhaṃ ayaṃ ca nitya iti
tasmāt sukhaḥ` — It is constant because it is permanent, because it is inexhaustible. It is blissful precisely because it is permanent: whatever is impermanent is suffering, but this is permanent; therefore it is blissful.

**J6. The verse-word is not treated as a generic synonym of the Buddha’s dharma-body. Sthiramati assigns vimuktikāya to the attainment of basis-transformation through the abandonment of dauṣṭhulya in śrāvakas, specifically through abandoning the kleśāvaraṇa.**
- chosen: vimuktikāyaḥ = the liberation-body associated with śrāvakas’ abandonment of the afflictive obstruction (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1525, 1526, 1527, 1528, 1529]: `āśrayaparāvṛttiḥ śrāvakādigatadauṣṭhulyahānitaś ca prāpyate yad āha
vimuktikāya iti
bodhisattvagatadauṣṭhulyahānitaś ca prāpyate yad āha
dharmākhyo 'yaṃ mahāmuner iti
dvidhā āvaraṇabhedena sottarā niruttarā cāśrayaparāvṛttir uktā` — Basis-transformation is also attained through abandoning the badness present in śrāvakas and so on; hence he says ‘liberation-body.’ It is also attained through abandoning the badness present in bodhisattvas; hence he says ‘this, of the Great Sage, is called Dharma.’ Thus, because of the difference of obstructions, basis-transformation has been stated as twofold: with something higher still, and unsurpassed.
- trbh [1551]: `kleśāvaraṇaprahāṇāc chrāvakāṇāṃ vimuktikāyaḥ` — Because of the abandonment of the afflictive obstruction, it is the liberation-body of the śrāvakas.

**J7. Although the compound dharmākhya literally means ‘called Dharma,’ Sthiramati’s explanation supplies the understood kāya and identifies it as the Mahāmuni’s dharmakāya, attained through the abandonment of both afflictive and cognitive obstructions. Mahāmuni is explicitly glossed as the Buddha Bhagavān.**
- chosen: dharmākhyaḥ ayaṃ mahāmuneḥ = ‘this, of the Great Sage, is called the Dharma-body’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1552, 1553, 1554, 1555]: `sa evāśrayaparāvṛttilakṣaṇo dharmākhyo 'py ucyate mahāmuner
bhūmipāramitābhāvanayā kleśajñeyāvaraṇaprahāṇāt
āśrayaparāvṛttisamudāgamān
mahāmuner dharmakāya ity ucyate` — That same thing, characterized as basis-transformation, is also called ‘Dharma’ for the Great Sage. Because, through cultivating the stages and perfections, there is abandonment of the afflictive and cognitive obstructions and the attainment of basis-transformation, it is called the dharma-body of the Great Sage.
- trbh [1556, 1557]: `saṃsārāparityāgāt tadanupasaṃkleśatvād
bodhisattvānāṃ sarvadharmavibhutvalābhataś ca dharmakāya ity ucyate` — Because there is no abandonment of saṃsāra, because there is no defilement by it, and because bodhisattvas obtain mastery over all dharmas, it is called the dharma-body.
- trbh [1558]: `mahāmuner iti paramauneyayogād buddho bhagavān mahāmunir iti` — ‘Of the Great Sage’: because of possessing supreme sagehood, the Buddha, the Blessed One, is the Great Sage.

## Translation

> That very basis-transformation (āśraya-parāvṛtti) is the uncontaminated element-source (anāsrava dhātu)—
inconceivable, wholesome, constant, blissful.
That, for the śrāvakas, is the liberation-body (vimuktikāya);
this, of the Great Sage (mahāmuni), is called the Dharma-body (dharmakāya).
