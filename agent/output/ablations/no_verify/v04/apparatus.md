## Justifications

**J1. The locative adverb tatra is not a vague ‘there’ or a reference to the immediately preceding noun vedanā; it is explicitly connected to ālayavijñāna, the topic already under discussion.**
- chosen: tatra = ‘there, in the ālayavijñāna’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [308]: `tatrety ālayavijñānam eva prakṛtatvāt saṃbadhyate` — ‘Tatra’ connects with ālayavijñāna alone, because that is the topic.

**J2. upekṣā is the technical neutral feeling associated with ālayavijñāna, not ordinary negligence or contempt; the commentary excludes sukha and duḥkha for this ālaya-feeling.**
- chosen: upekṣā vedanā = the feeling is equanimity, i.e. neither pleasure nor pain (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [309, 310, 311]: `upekṣaivālayavijñāne
vedanā na sukhā na duḥkhā tayoḥ paricchinnālambanākāratvād
rāgadveṣānuśayitatvāc ca` — In ālayavijñāna, the feeling is only upekṣā; it is not pleasure and not pain, because those two have a delimited object-aspect and are accompanied by latent attachment and aversion.

**J3. The compound is parsed as the technical moral classification of ālayavijñāna: anivṛta excludes nivṛta, and avyākṛta excludes wholesome and unwholesome. It is not merely ‘unexplained’.**
- chosen: anivṛtāvyākṛtam = unobscured and indeterminate; predicated of ālayavijñāna (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [312, 313]: `anivṛtāvyākṛtañ ca tat
ālayavijñānam iti prakṛtam` — ‘And that is anivṛtāvyākṛta’: ālayavijñāna is what is under discussion.
- trbh [314, 315, 316, 317, 318]: `tatrānivṛtagrahaṇaṃ nivṛtavyavacchedārtham
avyākṛtagrahaṇaṃ kuśalākuśalavyavacchedārtham
manobhūmikair āgantukair upakleśair anāvṛtatvād anivṛtam
vipākatvād vipākaṃ
prati kuśalākuśalatvenāvyākaraṇād avyākṛtam` — The mention of anivṛta serves to exclude nivṛta; the mention of avyākṛta serves to exclude wholesome and unwholesome. It is anivṛta because it is not covered by adventitious defilements belonging to the mental plane; it is avyākṛta because, being maturation, it is not classified as wholesome or unwholesome with respect to maturation.

**J4. tathā does not merely add ‘also contact, etc.’; Sthiramati spells out the analogy: just as ālayavijñāna is a maturation, has an undelimited object-aspect, is always conjoined with sparśa etc., and has neutral feeling and anivṛtāvyākṛta status, so too sparśa and the rest share the corresponding features.**
- chosen: tathā sparśādayaḥ = contact and the rest are likewise, with the same relevant ālaya-properties (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [319, 320, 321, 322, 323, 324, 325]: `tathā sparśādayaḥ
yathālayavijñānam ekāntena vipāko 'paricchinnālambanākāraṃ sadā sparśādibhir
anvitaṃ tatra copekṣaiva vedanānivṛtāvyākṛtañ ca tathā sparśādayo 'py ekāntena
vipākā evāparicchinnālambanākārāś ca
ātmānaṃ hitvetaraiś caturbhir
ālayavijñānena ca nityam anugatās teṣu copekṣaiva vedanānivṛtāvyākṛtāś
cālayavijñānavat` — ‘Likewise contact and the rest’: as ālayavijñāna is exclusively maturation, has an undelimited object-aspect, is always accompanied by contact and the rest, and in it the feeling is only upekṣā and it is anivṛtāvyākṛta, so too contact and the rest are exclusively maturations, have undelimited object-aspects, are always accompanied by the other four and by ālayavijñāna, and in them too the feeling is upekṣā and they are anivṛtāvyākṛta, just like ālayavijñāna.
- trbh [326, 327]: `na hi vipākena saṃprayuktānām avipākatvam
aparicchinnālambanākāreṇa ca paricchinnālambanākāratvaṃ saṃbhavati` — For what is conjoined with a maturation cannot be non-maturation; nor can what is conjoined with an undelimited object-aspect have a delimited object-aspect.

**J5. The second tat again refers to ālayavijñāna. The commentary explicitly rejects the view that one identical ālayavijñāna persists throughout saṃsāra; its continuing is by continuum/series, in keeping with momentariness.**
- chosen: tac ca vartate = this ālayavijñāna continues, but not as one unchanged entity (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [330, 331, 332, 333, 334, 335]: `kiṃ punas tad ālayavijñānam ekam abhinnam āsaṃsāram anuvartate
uta saṃtānena
na hi tad ekam abhinnam anuvartate kṣaṇikatvāt
kiṃ tarhi
tac ca vartate srotasaughavat
tac cety ālayavijñānam eva saṃbadhyate` — The question is whether that ālayavijñāna continues throughout saṃsāra as one undivided thing or by continuum. It certainly does not continue as one undivided thing, because it is momentary. What then? ‘And this goes on, like a stream/flood.’ In ‘tac ca’, ālayavijñāna alone is connected.

**J6. The printed srotasaughavat is segmented as srotasā + oghavat. The commentary defines srotas as uninterrupted cause-effect continuity and ogha as a continuous water-flow, then makes the yathā/evam simile explicit. Thus oghavat is comparative ‘like a flood’, not an adjective ‘possessing a strong stream’.**
- chosen: srotasā oghavat = by way of a stream/continuum, like a flood (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [336, 337, 338, 339]: `tatra sroto hetuphalayor
nairantaryaprabandhena pravṛttiḥ
udakasamūhasya pūrvāparabhāgāvicchedena
pravāha ogha ity ucyate` — Here ‘stream’ is the occurrence of cause and result in an uninterrupted continuum. The flow of a mass of water without a break between earlier and later parts is called ‘flood’.
- trbh [340, 341, 342]: `yathā hy oghas tṛṇakāṣṭhagomayādīn ākarṣayaṅ
gacchaty evam ālayavijñānam api puṇyāpuṇyāneñjyakarmavāsanānugataṃ
sparśamanaskārādīn ākarṣan srotasāsaṃsāram avyuparataṃ pravartata iti` — As a flood goes along drawing grass, wood, cow-dung and the like, so too ālayavijñāna, followed by the impressions of meritorious, unmeritorious and unchanging karma, draws contact, attention and the rest and proceeds without stopping by way of a stream throughout saṃsāra.

## Translation

> There, in the store-consciousness (ālayavijñāna),
the feeling (vedanā) is equanimity (upekṣā);
and that [store-consciousness] is unobscured-indeterminate (anivṛtāvyākṛta).
Contact and the rest (sparśādayaḥ) are likewise;
and this [store-consciousness] goes on, in a stream-continuum (srotas), like a flood (ogha).

## One-shot delta

- A commentary-blind translation might take vedanā from a dictionary as ‘pain’ and produce ‘indifference is pain’; Sthiramati instead defines this vedanā as the neutral feeling, neither sukha nor duḥkha.
- It might let tatra, tat, and tac ca float or attach them to vedanā or sparśādayaḥ; the commentary repeatedly fixes them to ālayavijñāna.
- It might render avyākṛta as simply ‘unexplained’ and miss the moral classification: excluded from both wholesome and unwholesome, and also anivṛta as not covered by adventitious mental defilements.
- It might take tathā as a loose ‘also’; the commentary makes it carry over a set of properties from ālayavijñāna to contact and the rest.
- It might read tac ca vartate as the persistence of one identical store-consciousness; Sthiramati explicitly rejects that and explains continuation by a momentary continuum.
- It might analyze srotasaughavat as a single adjective ‘having a strong stream’; the commentary supports srotasā + comparative oghavat, ‘by way of a stream, like a flood’.
