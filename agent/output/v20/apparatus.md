## Justifications

**J1. The verse is not about ‘options’ in the ordinary sense but about the endless plurality of vikalpas according to the difference of their construed objects, internal or external, even up to Buddha-dharmas.**
- chosen: ‘conceptual construction’ for vikalpa; ‘is construed/imagined’ for vikalpyate; the repeated yena yena / yad yad is distributive over an unlimited range of imagined objects. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1297, 1298, 1299, 1300]: `ādhyātmikabāhyavikalpyavastubhedena vikalpānām ānantyaṃ pradarśayann āha
yena yena vikalpeneti
yad yad vastu prakalpyate
ādhyātmikaṃ bāhyaṃ vāntaśo yāvad buddhadharmā api` — He says this while showing the infinitude of constructions through the difference of internal and external objects to be construed: ‘by whatever construction’; whatever object is projected/construed—internal or external, even as far as Buddha-dharmas.

**J2. Although neuter nominative and accusative are formally identical, the passive verb and Sthiramati’s restatement with prakalpyate make vastu the thing undergoing conceptual construction, not the object of an unexpressed active agent.**
- chosen: yad yad vastu is construed as the neuter nominative subject of the passive vikalpyate: ‘whatever object is construed’. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1299, 1304]: `yad yad vastu prakalpyate
yad vastu vikalpyaviṣayas tad yasmāt sattābhāvān na vidyate` — Whatever object is projected/construed; whatever object is the domain/object-to-be-construed, since, because of absence of being, it does not exist.

**J3. Sthiramati explicitly restates the verse as parikalpita-svabhāva and contrasts it with a hetu-pratyaya-pratibaddha-svabhāva, a nature bound to causes and conditions.**
- chosen: parikalpita evāsau svabhāvaḥ means ‘that nature is just the imagined (parikalpita) nature’, not a causally conditioned own-being. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1301, 1305, 1306, 1307]: `parikalpita evāsau svabhāva iti
tasmāt tad vastu
parikalpitasvabhāvam eva
na hetupratyayapratibaddhasvabhāvam` — ‘That nature is just the imagined,’ he says. Therefore that object has only an imagined nature, not a nature bound to causes and conditions.
- trbh [1313]: `sarvam idaṃ vikalpamātram eva tadarthasya parikalpitarūpatvāt` — All this is merely construction, because its referent has the form of what is imagined.

**J4. Sthiramati introduces this as the reason and explains the negation by sattā-abhāva, the absence of being. The sūtra quotation also uses vidyante existentially: dharmas do not exist in the way childish ordinary people grasp them.**
- chosen: na sa vidyate is existential: ‘it is not found / does not exist’, not merely ‘it is not known’. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1302, 1303, 1304]: `atra kāraṇam āha
na sa vidyata iti
yad vastu vikalpyaviṣayas tad yasmāt sattābhāvān na vidyate` — Here he states the reason: ‘it is not found/does not exist.’ Whatever object is the domain of construction, since, because of absence of being, it does not exist.
- trbh [1315]: `na khalu punaḥ subhūte dharmās tathā vidyante yathā bālapṛthagjanā abhiniviṣṭā` — For, Subhūti, dharmas do not exist in the way that childish ordinary people are attached to them.

**J5. Sthiramati argues from the observed arising of many mutually contradictory constructions with respect to one object or its absence, and rejects the possibility that one object or absence has many mutually contradictory natures.**
- chosen: The translation avoids implying a real substrate possessing many real contradictory natures; the referent is imagined, and all this is vikalpa-mātra. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1308, 1309, 1310, 1311, 1312, 1313]: `tathā hy
ekasmin vastuni tadabhāve ca parasparaviruddhānekavikalpapravṛttir dṛṣṭā
na
ca tad ekaṃ vastu tadabhāvo vā parasparaviruddhānekasvabhāvo yujyate
tasmāt
sarvam idaṃ vikalpamātram eva tadarthasya parikalpitarūpatvāt` — For thus, with respect to one object and also its absence, the occurrence of many mutually contradictory constructions is observed. And it is not tenable that that one object, or its absence, have many mutually contradictory natures. Therefore all this is merely construction, because its referent has an imagined form.

## Translation

> By whatever conceptual construction (vikalpa),
whatever object is construed,
that nature (svabhāva) is just the imagined (parikalpita);
it is not found to exist.

## Analyzer disagreements

- ByT5 gives vikalpyate with lemma/base vikalpay; I normalize it to the bare verbal root kḷp with prefix vi, as required by the apparatus format, while retaining present passive 3rd singular.
- ByT5 lemmatizes parikalpitaḥ as parikalpay; as a subanta in this verse its nominal stem is parikalpita, a participial adjective predicated of svabhāvaḥ.
- For yad yad and vastu I specify nominative singular rather than leaving the neuter nom./acc. ambiguity open, because the passive construction vikalpyate and Sthiramati’s gloss prakalpyate construe the object as the passive subject.

## One-shot delta

- A commentary-blind rendering might take vikalpa in the dictionary sense ‘option/alternative’; Sthiramati instead glosses the verb with prakalpyate and frames the verse as about limitless conceptual constructions.
- It might translate na sa vidyate as ‘it is not known’; Sthiramati’s sattā-abhāva explanation makes the point existential: it is not found to exist.
- It might miss the technical force of parikalpita-svabhāva and render only ‘it is imagined’; the commentary explicitly contrasts this with a cause-and-condition-bound nature.
- It might leave a real object as substrate for various descriptions; Sthiramati argues that mutually contradictory constructions cannot be real natures of one object and concludes vikalpa-mātra.
