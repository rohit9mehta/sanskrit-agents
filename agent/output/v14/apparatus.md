## Justifications

**J1. The term is not physical throwing but the mind’s being scattered away from the concentration-object through rāga, dveṣa, and moha.**
- chosen: vikṣepa is translated as mental “distraction/scattering.” (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [915, 916, 917, 918, 919, 920]: `vikṣepo rāgadveṣamohāṃśikaś cetaso visāraḥ
vividhaṃ kṣipyate 'nena cittam
iti vikṣepaḥ
yai rāgadveṣamohaiś cittaṃ samādhyālambanād bahiḥ kṣipyate teṣu
yathāsaṃbhavaṃ vikṣepaḥ prajñapyate
eṣa ca vairāgyaparipanthakarmaḥ` — Distraction is a scattering of the mind, an aspect of greed, hatred, and delusion: by it the mind is variously thrown about. Where greed, hatred, and delusion throw the mind outside the object of concentration, distraction is designated; it obstructs dispassion.

**J2. Sthiramati treats it as affliction-associated discernment by which bodily, verbal, and mental conduct proceeds uncomprehended through ignorance of what is to be done and not done.**
- chosen: asaṃprajanya is translated as “lack of clear comprehension.” (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [921, 922, 923, 924]: `asaṃprajanyaṃ kleśasaṃprayuktā prajñā yayāsaṃviditā kāyavākcittacaryā
abhikramapratikramādiṣu vartate karaṇīyākaraṇīyājñānāt
etac
cāpattisaṃniśrayadānakarmakam` — Asaṃprajanya is affliction-associated discernment by which bodily, verbal, and mental conduct goes on uncomprehended in going forth, returning, and the like, because of ignorance of what should and should not be done; it provides the basis for transgression.

**J3. Although derived from kukṛta, “badly done,” Sthiramati says that in this caitasika context it is mental vexation/remorse over a bad deed, not the deed or wickedness itself.**
- chosen: kaukṛtya is translated as “regret/remorse,” a mental factor. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [925, 926, 927, 928, 929, 930]: `kaukṛtyaṃ cetaso vipratisāraḥ
kutsitaṃ kṛtam iti kukṛtam
tadbhāvaḥ
kaukṛtyam
iha tu kukṛtaviṣayaś cetaso vilekhaḥ kaukṛtyaṃ caitasikādhikārāt
etac ca cittasthitiparipanthakarmakam` — Kaukṛtya is regret of the mind. What is badly done is kukṛta; its state is kaukṛtya. Here, however, because the topic is mental factors, kaukṛtya is a mental scraping/vexation whose object is a bad deed; it obstructs mental stability.
- trbh [971, 972, 973]: `tatrākuśalam akṛtvā kuśalañ ca kṛtvā yaś cetaso vilekhas tat saṃkliṣṭaṃ
kaukṛtyam
yat kuśalam akṛtvākuśalañ ca kṛtvā tat kaukṛtyam akliṣṭam` — Mental vexation over not having done the unwholesome and having done the wholesome is defiled regret; regret over not having done the wholesome and having done the unwholesome is undefiled.

**J4. The gloss makes it a contraction or shutdown of the mind’s autonomous operation toward objects, including non-operation through the sense-doors; this is more specific than generic sloth.**
- chosen: middha is translated as “torpor.” (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [931, 932, 933, 934, 935, 936, 937, 938, 939]: `middham asvatantravṛtticetaso 'bhisaṃkṣepaḥ
vṛttir ālambane pravṛttiḥ
sāsvatantrā cetaso yato bhavati tan middham
kāyasandhāraṇāsamarthā vā vṛttiś
cetaso 'svatantrā sā yato bhavati tan middham
abhisaṃkṣepaś cetasaś
cakṣurādīndriyadvāreṇāpravṛttiḥ
etac ca mohāṃśe prajñapanān mohāṃśikaṃ
kṛtyātipattisaṃniśrayadānakarmakañ ca` — Middha is the contraction of a mind whose activity is not autonomous. Activity is engagement with an object; that from which the mind’s activity becomes non-autonomous is middha. Or it is activity unable to sustain the body; that from which such non-autonomous mental activity arises is middha. The mind’s contraction is its non-operation through the eye and other sense-doors. Since it is designated within the delusion-aspect, it is delusional, and it provides the basis for neglecting what is to be done.

**J5. Sthiramati distinguishes vitarka as paryeṣaka manojalpa, a coarse searching operation, and vicāra as pratyavekṣaka manojalpa, a subtler examining/reviewing operation.**
- chosen: vitarka and vicāra are translated as “searching inner speech” and “examining inner speech.” (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [940, 941, 942, 943, 944, 945, 946]: `vitarkaḥ paryeṣako manojalpaḥ prajñācetanāviśeṣaḥ
paryeṣakaḥ kim etad iti
nirūpaṇākārapravṛttaḥ
manaso jalpo manojalpaḥ
jalpa iva jalpaḥ
jalpo
'rthakathanam` — Vitarka is searching mental talk, a particular form of discernment and volition. ‘Searching’ means proceeding in the form of determining ‘what is this?’ Mental talk is the mind’s talk; talk is so called because it is like speech, speech being the telling of a meaning.
- trbh [954, 955, 956, 957, 958, 959, 960, 961, 962]: `sa eva cittasyaudārikatā
audārikateti
sthūlatā vastumātraparyeṣaṇākāratvāt
eṣa ca nayo vicāre 'pi draṣṭavyaḥ
vicāro 'pi hi cetanāprajñāviśeṣātmakaḥ
pratyavekṣako manojalpa eva
idaṃ tad iti pūrvādhigatanirūpaṇāt
ata eva ca
cittasūkṣmatety ucyate` — That very thing is the coarseness of mind, called coarseness because its form is the mere search for the object. The same reasoning applies to vicāra too: vicāra is also of the nature of a particular volition and discernment; it is examining mental talk, because it determines what was previously grasped as ‘this is that.’ Therefore it is called subtlety of mind.

**J6. The phrase does not mean “in the two” as a locative singular. Sthiramati parses dvaye as a pair plus a pair, namely kaukṛtya–middha and vitarka–vicāra, and then glosses dvidhā as kliṣṭa and akliṣṭa.**
- chosen: dvaye is neuter dual: “the two dyads”; dvidhā means “twofold,” i.e. defiled and undefiled. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [966, 967, 968, 969, 970]: `dvaye dvidheti
dvayañ ca dvayañ ca dvaye
te punaḥ kaukṛtyamiddhe vitarkavicārau ca
ete ca
catvāro dharmā dvidhā kliṣṭā akliṣṭāś ca` — As for ‘two dyads, twofold’: a dyad and a dyad are the two dyads. These are regret-and-torpor and vitarka-and-vicāra. These four dharmas are twofold: defiled and undefiled.

**J7. The verse’s upakleśāḥ is therefore not to be read as making every occurrence of regret, torpor, vitarka, and vicāra afflicted; Sthiramati explicitly excludes their undefiled forms.**
- chosen: For the two dyads, only their defiled forms count as upakleśas. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [976, 977, 978, 979, 980, 981]: `kāmavyāpādavihiṃsādivitarkāḥ kliṣṭāḥ
naiśkramyādivitarkā akliṣṭāḥ
evaṃ
paropaghātopāyavicāraḥ kliṣṭaḥ
parānugrahopāyavicāro 'kliṣṭaḥ
tatra ye kaukṛtyamiddhavitarkavicārāḥ kliṣṭās ta evopakleśā netare` — Vitarkas such as desire, malice, and violence are defiled; vitarkas such as renunciation are undefiled. Likewise, vicāra concerning a means of harming another is defiled, while vicāra concerning a means of benefiting another is undefiled. Among regret, torpor, vitarka, and vicāra, those that are defiled are upakleśas, not the others.

## Translation

> Distraction (vikṣepa), lack of clear comprehension (asaṃprajanya),
regret (kaukṛtya), and torpor (middha) too;
searching inner speech (vitarka) and examining inner speech (vicāra):
secondary afflictions (upakleśa).
The two dyads—regret/torpor and searching/examining—are twofold;
only their defiled forms count as such.

- It could read dvaye as locative singular, “in the two,” but Sthiramati parses it as neuter dual: two dyads, kaukṛtya–middha and vitarka–vicāra.
