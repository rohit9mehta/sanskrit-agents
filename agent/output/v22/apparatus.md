## Justifications

**J1. Translate the first half as a statement that the perfected is neither different nor non-different from the other-dependent, not as a statement about the dependent being different from something else or about mere ‘dependence’ in the abstract.**
- chosen: The first saḥ is the perfected nature (pariniṣpannaḥ), and paratantrataḥ means ‘from / in relation to the other-dependent nature (paratantraḥ).’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1342, 1343, 1344, 1345, 1346, 1347, 1348]: `ata eva sa naiveti parikalpitena svabhāvena paratantrasya sadā rahitatā
pariniṣpannaḥ
rahitatā ca dharmatā
sā ca dharmatā dharmān nānyā nānanyā
yujyate
pariniṣpannaś ca paratantradharmatety ataḥ paratantrāt pariniṣpanno
nānyo nānanya iti boddhavyaḥ` — On ‘for just this reason he is not ...’: the perfected is the constant absence, in the other-dependent, of the imagined nature. Absence is dharmatā, and that dharmatā is not properly either different or non-different from the dharmas. And since the perfected is the dharmatā of the other-dependent, the perfected should be understood as neither different nor non-different from the other-dependent.

**J2. Preserve the double denial rather than resolving it into simple identity, simple difference, or a vague ‘indescribability.’**
- chosen: Neither ‘different’ nor ‘non-different’ is to be affirmed without qualification. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1349, 1350, 1351, 1352, 1353, 1354, 1355]: `yadi hi pariniṣpannaḥ paratantrād anyaḥ syād
evaṃ na parikalpitena paratantraḥ śūnyaḥ syāt
athānanya evam api
pariniṣpanno na viśuddhālambanaḥ syāt paratantravat saṃkleśātmakatvāt
evaṃ
paratantraś ca na kleśātmakaḥ syāt
pariniṣpannād ananyatvāt pariniṣpannavat` — If the perfected were different from the other-dependent, then the other-dependent would not be empty of the imagined. But if it were simply non-different, then the perfected would not be a purified object, because, like the other-dependent, it would have the nature of defilement; and likewise the other-dependent would not have the nature of defilement, because, being non-different from the perfected, it would be like the perfected.

**J3. Take vācyo with an explicit ellipsis supplied by Sthiramati, not as ‘expressible like impermanence’ in a loose lexical sense.**
- chosen: anityatādivat vācyo means ‘is to be stated [as neither different nor non-different], as with impermanence and the rest.’ (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1356, 1357]: `anityatādivad vācyo
nānyo nānanya iti vākyaśeṣaḥ` — ‘Is to be stated as with impermanence and the rest’: the remainder of the sentence is ‘neither different nor non-different.’
- trbh [1358, 1359, 1360, 1361, 1362, 1363, 1364, 1365]: `yathā hy anityatā duḥkhatānātmatā ca
saṃskārādibhyo
nānyā nānanyā
yadi saṃskārebhyo 'nityatā anyā
evaṃ tarhi saṃskārā nityāḥ
syuḥ
athānanyā evam api saṃskārāḥ pradhvaṃsābhāvarūpāḥ syur anityatāvat
evaṃ duḥkhatādiṣv api vācyam` — For just as impermanence, sufferingness, and selflessness are neither different nor non-different from formations and the like: if impermanence were different from formations, then formations would be permanent; but if it were non-different, then formations would have the nature of destruction-absence, like impermanence. The same is to be said for sufferingness and the rest.

**J4. Translate the last pāda as a locative-absolute condition: ‘when this perfected nature has not been seen, that other-dependent nature is not seen.’**
- chosen: In nādṛṣṭe ’smin sa dṛśyate, asmin refers to the perfected nature, while saḥ refers to the other-dependent nature. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1366, 1367, 1368, 1369, 1370]: `yadi grāhyagrāhakabhāvarahitaḥ paratantraḥ katham asau gṛhyate agṛhyamāṇo vā
katham astīti vijñāyate
ata āha
nādṛṣṭe 'smin sa dṛśyate
nādṛṣṭe 'sminn iti pariniṣpannasvabhāve sa dṛśyata iti paratantraḥ svabhāvaḥ` — Objection: if the other-dependent is devoid of the condition of grasper and grasped, how is it apprehended? Or, if it is not apprehended, how is it known to exist? Therefore he says: ‘When this has not been seen, it is not seen.’ In ‘when this has not been seen’ the reference is to the perfected nature; in ‘it is seen’ the reference is to the other-dependent nature.
- trbh [1371, 1372, 1373, 1374]: `nirvikalpalokottarajñānadṛśye pariniṣpanne svabhāve adṛṣṭe apratividdhe
asākṣātkṛte tatpṛṣṭhalabdhaśuddhalaukikajñānagamyatvāt paratantro 'nyena
jñānena na gṛhyate
ataḥ pariniṣpanne 'dṛṣṭe paratantro na dṛśyate` — When the perfected nature, which is seen by non-conceptual supramundane knowledge, has not been seen, penetrated, or directly realized, the other-dependent is not grasped by any other knowledge, because it is accessible to the pure worldly knowledge obtained subsequently to that. Therefore, when the perfected has not been seen, the other-dependent is not seen.

**J5. Maintain the restricted scope: it is not seen by ‘another’ cognition before the perfected is realized; it is seen by subsequently obtained pure worldly knowledge.**
- chosen: The negation in the final pāda is not a denial that the other-dependent can ever be seen. (depends on commentary: **yes**)
- trbh [1375, 1376]: `na punar
lokottarajñānapṛṣṭhalabdhenāpi jñānena na dṛśyate` — But it is not the case that it is not seen even by the knowledge obtained subsequent to supramundane knowledge.
- trbh [1378, 1379, 1380, 1381, 1382, 1383, 1384, 1385]: `tat pṛṣṭhalabdhena jñānena māyāmarīcisvapnapratiśrutkodakacandranirmitasamān
sarvadharmān pratyetīti
atra ca dharmāḥ paratantrasaṃgṛhītā abhipretāḥ
pariniṣpannaś cākāśavad
ekarasaḥ
jñānañ ca yathoktaṃ
nirvikalpena jñānenākāśasamatayā tān sarvadharmān paśyatīti
paratantradharmāṇāṃ tathatāmātradarśanāt` — With the knowledge obtained subsequently to that, one understands all dharmas to be like illusion, mirage, dream, echo, moon in water, and magical creation. Here the dharmas intended are those included in the other-dependent. And the perfected is of one taste, like space. And knowledge is as stated: by non-conceptual knowledge one sees all those dharmas as equal to space, because one sees only the suchness of other-dependent dharmas.

## Translation

> For just this reason, it is neither different
nor non-different from the other-dependent (paratantra);
as with impermanence and the rest, it is to be so stated.
When this perfected nature (pariniṣpanna) has not been seen,
that other-dependent nature is not seen.

## Analyzer disagreements

- paratantrataḥ: the analyzer’s api segmentation normalized the word to paratantrāt, and the local tag effectively treats it as an ablatival form of paratantra. The printed bhāṣya reading is paratantrataḥ; I analyze it as a tasil indeclinable with ablatival force, while noting that Sthiramati glosses it by paratantrāt at trbh 1347–1348.

- adṛṣṭe: the analyzer’s local output separates na + a + dṛṣṭe, treating a as independent and dṛṣṭe as positive. I read na + adṛṣṭe, where adṛṣṭe is the privative/nañ PPP ‘unseen,’ explicitly glossed by adṛṣṭe apratividdhe asākṣātkṛte at trbh 1371–1372.

## One-shot delta

- A commentary-blind translation might take both occurrences of saḥ as the same referent; Sthiramati explicitly distinguishes them: first pariniṣpannaḥ, later paratantraḥ.
- It might render anityatādivad vācyo as ‘expressible like impermanence,’ missing Sthiramati’s supplied ellipsis: ‘to be stated as neither different nor non-different.’
- It might construe nādṛṣṭe ’smin as ‘when this dependent nature is unseen,’ but the commentary makes asmin the perfected nature and saḥ the other-dependent nature.
- It might take the last negation absolutely—‘it is not seen’—whereas Sthiramati restricts it: the other-dependent is not grasped by any other cognition before realization of the perfected, but is seen by subsequently obtained pure worldly knowledge.
